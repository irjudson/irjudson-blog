*This is the first time I've tried to say publicly what Buffalo Jump Ranch actually is. Montana Festival gave me the frame.*

---

I went to Montana Festival to build business for Buffalo Jump Forge. Two days in Bozeman, 1,000 people, as many conversations as my voice would hold. The business part worked fine.

The rest of it I wasn't expecting.

## George Packer's Four Americas

During his talk, Packer referenced [his Atlantic piece on the Four Americas](https://ww1.up.edu/garaventa/files/fildg%20files/the-four-americas.pdf). The frame is four ways of being American that don't talk to each other: Free America, Smart America, Real America, and Just America.

What stopped me was his description of Just America: it's organized around *grievance*. Not aspiration. The moral engine is a catalogue of wrongs. Identity is built from who has been wronged and by whom.

I've been trying to name something since 2016. That was most of it.

## Yo-Yo Ma and the Bison

Later that day, Cristina Mormorunni and Kirk Johnson did a panel on bison and Indigenous land stewardship. Cristina screened a film she made of Yo-Yo Ma playing Amazing Grace as bison were returned to the Blackfeet Nation — coming home to their brother and sister after more than a century.

Together with Packer's frame, that image finished a thought I'd been working on for a long time.

We're running on the urgency of fear. Fear of what we're losing, fear of what's coming. It works, up to a point — fear is urgent, fear mobilizes. But it doesn't build anything. And eventually it eats you.

The urgency of hope is harder. It requires believing what you're building matters, not just that what you're fighting against is wrong. It requires having something to invite people *into*.

## What the Ranch Is For

Buffalo Jump Ranch is 150 acres in the lower Madison Valley, 45 minutes west of Bozeman. Buffalo Jump Forge lives there. So does something I haven't said publicly before.

The Ranch is a place to retreat into the vast quiet of rural Montana — not metaphorically, literally. A place to stop and be somewhere. The valley is big and the noise falls away faster than you'd expect.

We run creative workshops. The premise: nobody is the expert in everything, and everyone is humble enough to learn from someone else. You come to teach what you know and leave having learned something you didn't.

The elk, the birds, the fish in the creek — they're not backdrop. They're part of the community we're in out there.

What I want the Ranch to be is a place for people who want to do the actual work of turning that fear into something else. Enough quiet and enough community to remember what you're actually doing this for.

## Why I Came Back

I moved back to Montana 18 months ago after 10 years in Seattle. I went to the festival to build business. I came back knowing what I'm building it for.
