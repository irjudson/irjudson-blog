## The Reality of Technical Hiring

Let's start with how hiring actually works:

**You apply → Recruiter searches for you → They land on your GitHub → 10 seconds of scanning → Decision made**

That 10-second window determines whether you get a deeper look or get passed over. It's not fair, but it's reality.

**What recruiters are looking for in those 10 seconds:**

1. **Can I understand what you do?** (Clear bio, current role, tech stack)
2. **Are you currently active?** (Recent commits, maintained projects)
3. **Can I contact you?** (LinkedIn link, email, website)
4. **Do you have relevant experience?** (Work history, project descriptions)
5. **Is this a real engineer?** (Quality repositories, not just forks)

If your profile doesn't answer these questions immediately, you're invisible.

## The Three Things That Actually Matter

After tracking metrics from 1000+ profiles and monitoring my own during job search, three things consistently drive results:

### 1. LinkedIn Integration (Non-Negotiable)

Your GitHub profile must link to LinkedIn. Your LinkedIn must link back to GitHub.

Recruiters work across both platforms. Make the connection explicit or lose opportunities.

**Impact:** 3-5x increase in profile views when properly integrated.

**How to do it:**
- Add LinkedIn URL to your GitHub profile links
- Add GitHub URL to your LinkedIn contact info
- Mention GitHub activity in your LinkedIn About section
- Cross-reference projects between platforms

This isn't optional. This is table stakes.

### 2. Clear Work Experience Timeline

Not just "Senior Engineer at Company X." Actual context:

- What you built
- Scale you operated at
- Impact you had
- Technologies you used

Frame it in terms that matter to people evaluating you.

**Example - Bad:**
```
Senior Software Engineer at TechCorp
```

**Example - Good:**
```
Senior Software Engineer at TechCorp
Built real-time data processing pipeline handling 10M+ events/day
Stack: Python, Kafka, PostgreSQL, Kubernetes
Led team of 4 engineers, reduced processing latency by 60%
```

See the difference? One tells the recruiter nothing. The other tells them exactly what you can do.

### 3. Active Presence Indicators

You need evidence that you're current, not someone who was relevant five years ago.

**What signals active presence:**
- Recent contributions (green squares that aren't empty)
- Maintained repositories (updated within last 3-6 months)
- Ongoing technical engagement (issues, PRs, discussions)
- Current work reflected (not outdated bio or stale projects)

You don't need to be a daily contributor. You need to show you're actively writing code.

**Everything else—badges, stats, animations—is optional decoration.** These three are the foundation.

Here's what actually happened when I optimized my profile during my job search:

Knowing your profile is working for you reduces the pressure of active searching:
- You're not just sending applications into the void
- Opportunities come to you while you're working
- Your technical work is visible and valued
- You're building long-term career infrastructure

It's a small psychological win that makes job searching less stressful.

## What Won't Work (Common Mistakes)

**1. Badges and stats without substance**

Having 47 technology badges looks impressive until someone realizes you have no actual projects using those technologies.

Substance first, decoration second.

**2. Forked repositories with no contributions**

Your profile showing 50 repositories—all forks with no commits—signals "not actually working on things."

Quality over quantity. Show what *you* built.

**3. Empty or generic bio**

"Software engineer who likes to code" tells recruiters nothing.

"Backend engineer specializing in distributed systems, 8 years experience scaling Python/PostgreSQL applications to millions of users" tells them everything.

**4. No LinkedIn integration**

This is the fastest way to be invisible. Recruiters search both platforms. Connect them or lose 50%+ of opportunities.

**5. Stale presence**

Last commit 2 years ago? Last updated role from 3 jobs ago? Signals "not currently active in tech."

Keep it current or you're filtering yourself out.

GitHub is one piece of your technical presence. Complete the loop:

## When to Optimize (Hint: Now)

Don't wait until you're actively job searching:

**Optimize your profile when:**
- You're employed and happy (best leverage)
- You just completed a major project (fresh accomplishments)
- You're learning new tech (show current growth)

**Worst time to optimize:**

Your profile should always be working for you, not just when you need it.
